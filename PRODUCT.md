# Product

## Register

brand

**Bold.** The site should feel like the work of an operator who is unusually good at this and knows it — confident, distinctive, and technically impressive on first contact, not quiet or generic. Boldness is expressed through conviction of craft: a committed visual point of view, a strong voice, and signature moments a visitor remembers.

Boldness is **never** expressed through the claims. The register is loud about *how* the work is presented and disciplined about *what* is asserted — every result, metric, or outcome still has to be real and inspectable (see Design Principles). A bold surface over honest substance is the target; spectacle that inflates the substance is the failure.

> Historical note: earlier this file mandated evidence-led *restraint* site-wide. That was deliberately overridden — restraint kept reading as timid and generic and fought the goal of a site that impresses. Restraint now applies to claims, not to craft. Do not "restore restraint" as a blanket rule.

## Users

Several buyers, reached through four distinct offers, sharing one trait: they run real operations and want practical improvement, not AI experimentation for its own sake — more done with less chasing, recurring work that stops slipping, and a clear view of where processes get stuck.

- **Small professional offices** (law first, then adjacent practices) — the flagship buyer. An **owner** who decides whether to invest and an **operations lead** who feels the daily friction first. They want the office to run with less chasing: briefings ready, inbox triaged, documents moving, and a person still approving what matters.
- **Small teams with a stuck workflow** — the sprint buyer. Wants one concrete process mapped and a few automations built quickly, with no infrastructure commitment.
- **Founders and professionals who need a content presence** — the LinkedIn-engine buyer. Wants a running content pipeline without doing it themselves.
- **R&D and engineering leaders** — the enablement buyer. Wants their people fluent in AI-assisted coding and agent workflows, taught by someone who has led R&D.

Part of this audience reads **Hebrew** and works right-to-left. Hebrew is a real audience to design for, not an afterthought. The work is delivered in Hebrew and English where the business needs it (an operating capability); the website is fully bilingual — every page has an English version and a Hebrew (RTL) version under `/he`.

## Product Purpose

y[AI]r studio helps small professional offices and teams put AI to work on the operations they actually run — with a person kept visibly in charge of the decisions that matter. The site sells four things, and should make the right starting point obvious in four choices or fewer:

1. **Managed AI Office Assistant (flagship).** An ongoing service that runs an office's recurring work — morning briefings, email triage, document workflows, and the items waiting for an owner's approval — inside the client's own private environment. Priced as a setup fee plus a monthly retainer. This is the headline the homepage leads with.
2. **AI Workflow Sprint.** A fixed-scope engagement that maps one workflow and builds a small set of working automations for a team — the fastest, lowest-commitment way in, and the natural on-ramp to the managed assistant.
3. **LinkedIn Content Engine.** A managed content pipeline, already running on the studio itself, offered as a service with a per-client profile.
4. **AI Enablement Workshops.** Hands-on workshops in AI-assisted coding and agent workflows for R&D and engineering teams — the mentorship surface, drawing on real R&D-leadership experience.

The site should make what is for sale unmistakable, route each visitor to the one offer that fits, and show — not assert — that the work produces observable improvement with human judgment kept in the loop.

## Brand Personality

Confident, sharp, and technically formidable — an operator who has clearly done this at a high level and presents the work with conviction. The experience should impress on first contact and still feel accountable: a real person stays visibly responsible for the decisions that matter, and nothing runs fully unattended.

Confidence is carried two ways at once. **Craft** is asserted boldly — a distinctive look, a strong voice, signature moments. **Results** are demonstrated, never asserted — carried by inspectable artifacts and observable change, not by adjectives. The two never blur: a bold frame is credible only because the substance inside it is honest.

## Anti-references

Avoid the generic AI-agency and SaaS playbook — not because it is bold, but because it is *empty* bold: glowing gradients, decorative glass panels, vague futuristic imagery for its own sake, excessive monospace styling, repetitive card grids, and unsupported claims about "transformation." The failure mode to avoid is spectacle standing in for substance. Boldness earned by real craft and backed by real proof is the goal; boldness borrowed from clichés, or used to dress up claims we cannot demonstrate, is the thing to avoid.

## Design Principles

Each principle carries a *tell* — the concrete signal that it is actually being followed, not merely stated.

1. **Show improvement, do not merely claim it.** *Tell:* an improvement claim is backed by something the reader can inspect — a mapped workflow, a before/after structure, a real (if anonymized) artifact — not an adjective. Evidence belongs only where there is something real to show; pages without proof must not manufacture it. **Tie-breaker:** when this principle tempts toward a metric or result we cannot honestly demonstrate, accuracy wins — omit the proof rather than fabricate it.
2. **Lead with the operational problem and outcome before the technology.** *Tell:* a page opens on a business pain or a result; the AI and tooling enter only after the reader recognizes the problem as their own.
3. **Make complex workflows legible without oversimplifying them.** *Tell:* a workflow is shown as a real sequence with its decision and approval points intact, never flattened into a three-step cartoon.
4. **Be boldly distinctive; earn it with substance.** *Tell:* every page carries a committed point of view — a signature moment, a strong frame, a confident voice — and none of that boldness is spent inflating a claim. Boldness is applied to *how* something is shown; discipline to *what* is asserted. A page that is bland fails this principle; so does a page whose boldness outruns its evidence. **Tie-breaker:** if a bold treatment can be justified only by a claim we cannot back, cut the claim, not the craft — find a bold way to show what is actually true.
5. **Keep human judgment and approval visible within automated systems.** *Tell:* every depiction of an automated flow shows where a person approves or decides; automation never appears to run end to end on its own.

## Accessibility

Target and verify WCAG 2.2 AA — a checked result, not an aspiration: readable contrast (the measured token values live in the stylesheet, not here), visible keyboard focus, reduced-motion support, and plain language. Both reading directions are first-class — English (LTR) and Hebrew (RTL) — so the Hebrew-reading audience named in Users gets a genuinely native experience, not a mirrored afterthought.
