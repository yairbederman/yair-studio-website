# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Two buyers, often inside the same business: the **owner**, who decides whether to invest, and the **operations leader**, who runs the work day to day and feels the friction first. Both want practical improvement to real workflows, not AI experimentation for its own sake — more done with less chasing, recurring work that stops slipping, and a clear view of where processes get stuck.

Part of this audience reads **Hebrew** and works right-to-left. Hebrew is a real audience to design for, not an afterthought; the site serves English today, with a dedicated Hebrew homepage.

## Product Purpose

y[AI]r studio helps the businesses these buyers run map, improve, and optimize real workflows through process mapping, dashboards, automation, and internal AI systems. The website should make the available entry points clear and show that the work produces observable improvement, with human judgment kept in the loop.

The offers are entry points into one system, not four separate services: an AI workflow audit, internal AI systems, a dashboard and automation layer, and content and ad operations. **Which offer is the primary wedge — the one the site leads with — is an open strategic question, deliberately left undecided here.** Until it is decided, the site presents the offers as peers.

## Brand Personality

Precise, evidence-led, improvement-oriented, and accountable. The experience should feel pragmatic and technically capable, like an operations partner who keeps a person visibly responsible for the decisions that matter. Confidence is **demonstrated, not asserted** — carried by clear processes and observable change, not by adjectives or spectacle.

## Anti-references

Avoid generic AI-agency and SaaS aesthetics: glowing gradients, decorative glass panels, vague futuristic imagery, excessive monospace styling, repetitive card grids, and unsupported claims about transformation. Avoid visual polish that makes the work feel less concrete or less trustworthy.

## Design Principles

Each principle carries a *tell* — the concrete signal that it is actually being followed, not merely stated.

1. **Show improvement, do not merely claim it.** *Tell:* an improvement claim is backed by something the reader can inspect — a mapped workflow, a before/after structure, a real artifact — not an adjective. Evidence belongs only where there is something real to show; pages without proof must not manufacture it. **Tie-breaker:** when this principle tempts toward a metric or result we cannot honestly demonstrate, accuracy wins — omit the proof rather than fabricate it.
2. **Lead with the operational problem and outcome before the technology.** *Tell:* a page opens on a business pain or a result; the AI and tooling enter only after the reader recognizes the problem as their own.
3. **Make complex workflows legible without oversimplifying them.** *Tell:* a workflow is shown as a real sequence with its decision and approval points intact, never flattened into a three-step cartoon.
4. **Use restraint to communicate accuracy, not anonymity.** *Tell:* restraint reads as deliberate — one signal color, hairline structure, generous space — and still carries a clear point of view, never a blank or generic template.
5. **Keep human judgment and approval visible within automated systems.** *Tell:* every depiction of an automated flow shows where a person approves or decides; automation never appears to run end to end on its own.

## Accessibility

Target and verify WCAG 2.2 AA — a checked result, not an aspiration: readable contrast (the measured token values live in the stylesheet, not here), visible keyboard focus, reduced-motion support, and plain language. Both reading directions are first-class — English (LTR) and Hebrew (RTL) — so the Hebrew-reading audience named in Users gets a genuinely native experience, not a mirrored afterthought.
